## Gauss-Seidel Player Order and Equilibrium Selection (2026-04-17)

Across 8 runs with different player sweep orders, the model exhibits multiple Nash equilibria.
The sweep order acts as an equilibrium selection device: in the CH-first runs (3 out of 8),
r_strat stabilised below 1% for 7–9 consecutive iterations, constituting practical convergence.
CH-last runs failed to formally converge within 30 iterations, though residuals remained low
(1.3–3.7%). This is interpretable as the algorithm struggling to pin down a different basin of
attraction, not as evidence that no equilibrium exists.

## China-First Convergence Pattern (2026-04-17)

All 3 robustly converged runs have China (CH, ~931 GW, dominant player) solving first in the
Gauss-Seidel sweep. This suggests CH acts as a market anchor: when the largest player commits
first, all others best-respond to a stable reference, enabling the algorithm to settle. This is
an algorithmic property, distinct from the strategic last-mover advantage. For the paper: the
CH-first equilibrium can be presented as the focal/algorithmically robust equilibrium given
China's market dominance.

## US–APAC Role Swap Across Equilibria (2026-04-17)

The two most divergent equilibria (CH-first vs CH-last with the original order) show a complete
role reversal between US and APAC: in one equilibrium US expands to 122 GW by 2040 while APAC
shrinks to 51 GW; in the other, APAC expands to 300 GW while US exits the market entirely (0 GW).
Welfare differences are large: US welfare +19%, APAC welfare -22% between the two equilibria.
This multiplicity is economically meaningful and worth discussing as a coordination/equilibrium
selection problem in the paper.

## Price Uniqueness with Capacity Multiplicity (2026-04-17)

Across the 3 converged CH-first runs, equilibrium market prices (lambda) are identical for CH,
US, APAC, and ROW down to the decimal, despite substantial differences in capacity allocation
(e.g. US Kcap 2040 ranges from ~0 to 119 GW across runs). This suggests the price equilibrium
is effectively unique — pinned by the dominant players' cost structures and demand — while the
capacity allocation equilibrium is non-unique. Multiple quantity strategies support the same
price vector. For the paper: this is a useful robustness result — the policy-relevant outcomes
(market prices, welfare) are stable across equilibria even though the structural outcome (who
holds capacity) is not. Framing: "price equilibrium is unique; capacity distribution is
indeterminate among mid-tier players."

## Proximal Penalty Calibration — Evidence from Non-Convergence (2026-04-17)

The fact that 5 out of 8 runs fail to converge even as proximal penalties ramp to their final
values (c_pen_p=3, c_pen_dk=2) is actually evidence that the penalties are well-calibrated.
If penalties were too high, all runs would converge trivially — players could barely move, so
strategy changes would vanish mechanically rather than because a true fixed point was reached.
The persistent non-convergence in CH-last runs indicates genuine strategic dynamics are still
active at the penalty level used, meaning the 3 converged runs reflect a real equilibrium
rather than a penalty-induced artefact. This should be noted in the computational appendix as
a robustness argument for the penalty schedule.

## Efficiency vs. Strategic Stability Across Equilibria (2026-04-17)

Run 3 (ch,row,apac,us,eu,af) yields the highest total welfare (12,473 model units) and is also
the equilibrium where US exits (~0.2 GW by 2040), on top of EU and AF already exiting. This is
economically consistent: when higher-cost producers exit, demand is served entirely by lower-cost
producers (CH, APAC, ROW), producing a more efficient allocation and higher aggregate surplus.
This creates a clear tension for the paper between efficiency (total welfare maximised under
market concentration in cheap-cost regions) and strategic stability / political sustainability
(equilibria where more players retain capacity may be preferred even at lower aggregate welfare).
The model thus endogenously generates a trade-off between allocative efficiency and market
diversity that is relevant for policy discussions on solar supply chain concentration.

## Persistent Residual in max_abs_dp (2026-04-17)

All runs show a persistent max_abs_dp of ~2–5 $/kW that does not vanish, even when r_strat
converges. This is driven by a systematic structural drift in ROW and US capacity decisions
(ROW decommissioning, US adjusting), not by oscillation. The r_strat criterion is the more
economically meaningful convergence measure here; the absolute price criterion is dominated
by these slow-moving structural adjustments. This distinction should be noted in the
computational appendix.
